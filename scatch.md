Can't rely on LLMs to reason
What LLMs are useful for is syntactic pattern matching ... finind patterns in the order/arrangement of words in text. This does enhance semantic performance (geometric distances help determine which meaning of a word) but this is literally a surface feature not the semantics (inferences allowed from axioms) that can be used to define and then reason (deductively (induction and abduction are special cases of)) over a domain (of knowledge encoded in the axioms and L, inference rules).

Use LLMs, but verify their output
- make sure semantics make sense, close enough isn't correct sometimes especially when summarizing, put maybe when doing eDiscover (this is a good match, but may not be relevant b/c I can't use it in an argument (think Google search, might match but may not be relevant))

Use automated reasoners, but verify their rules
- Think 'calculator' or 'spreahsheet formula' ... GIGO, can't blame the calculator. You can only blame the calculator if it was programmed with incorrect rules of arithmetic, then it's the calculators fault.

Essentially it's a loop, use the pattern matching to find and encode inputs into a formal L, reason about it in determnistic ways on 'known good' rules, 

AR gives you proofs or gaurantees of compliance (output) given a set of rules and state (inputs), LLMs give you probable generation (output tokens) given context (input tokens).

This is exactly why you want determinism from experts. You go to expert to make sure you’re getting the authoritative answer. That is you want to have a deterministic outcome when you go see a expert. The expert very well may provide an answer as a probability. But that is a deterministic probability, meaning that the model from which the probability was inferred is a mathematical and thus deterministic process. That is, given the same set of inputs that model will always produce the same probability or forecast. Further, the expert should be able to explain the model itself and how it works. An acutal expert has the domain knowledge and formal methods skills to encode/decode that knowledge into words/numbers.

Most words are not encoded into formal languages for deterministic rigor of domains or reasoning over said domain.

What about 'knowledge graphs' ... these are stores of knowledge with rule-like predicates connecting them, as 'matter of facts' ... they aren't inference rules, but can be querried to find linked relationships "what are all the songs Dan has liked", "which songs are liked by Dan, Ed, and Michelle", and so forth. It cannot answer, if "Dan likes a song and Ed likes the same song, does Michelle like it?". You can build a statistical distrubution of likes, and try to infer a probability ... or build rules about the semantics of likes (relative to these people?) and use GOFAI. Building rules is hard, manual and tedious, and requires knowledge of encoding (building probabalistic models is hard too, and requires math/parameter encoding skills) ... but, when you're an expert and focussed on a single trackable issue , with some help from LLM enhanced corpus review, decision tree design, the rules can be captured, validated, and encoded in a very reasonable level of time and effort.

There's also the additional benefit of establishing formal 'records' of your reasoning to review and run through test of provable qualities such as consistency, completeness, satisfiability, soundness, etc.
